Research Vision

I'm drawn to questions about how transformer models work from the inside — how their internal computations form, why they behave the way they do on particular inputs, and how we can influence those behaviors at inference time. I don't see myself as specialized in one narrow corner of interpretability; I follow questions where there's something genuinely unclear and where I think careful analysis can help.

Why Linear Interpretability Works: Invariant Subspaces from Architectural Constraints
Andrés Sáurez, Yousung Lee, Dongsoo Har Under Review · NeurIPS 2026
Why do linear probes, sparse autoencoders, and activation steering consistently recover the same structure from transformer representations? We argue this is a consequence of the readout interface, not cross-entropy alone: linear unembedding makes every logit margin a projection of the residual stream onto a fixed contrast direction, while nonlinear readouts remove this necessity. A grokking ablation confirms the prediction—Fourier (circular) embeddings emerge under linear or 2-layer readouts and vanish under deeper nonlinear ones. From a sparse-superposition projection-error bound we derive the Self-Reference Property: a class prompt's own activation acts as a label-free reference for its feature direction, enabling zero-shot classification in a single forward pass, with SAE overlap analysis confirming class-token and instance activations recruit the same dictionary atoms. Validated across 8 classification tasks, 4 model families (LLaMA3-8B, Mistral-7B, GPT2-Small, LLaMA3.2-3B), and 3 architectures (decoder-only LLMs, BERT, ViT/LLaVA). Extended to circuit attribution: the projection-based variant SRP-BP outperforms AtP and AtP* on IOI in GPT-2 small, with predicted vs. observed direct logit attribution matching at slope 1.07, R2=0.92.

Circuit Fingerprints: How Answer Tokens Encode Their Geometrical Path
Andrés Sáurez, Neha Sengar, Dongsoo Har Preprint
Circuit discovery and activation steering have developed as separate research threads—we show they follow a single geometric principle. Answer tokens, processed in isolation, encode the computational pathways that produce them (Circuit Fingerprint Hypothesis), enabling gradient-free circuit discovery from geometric alignment alone. Validated on standard benchmarks (IOI, SVA, MCQA) using CPR/CMD metrics (MIB) across GPT2-Small, Qwen2.5-0.5B, Llama3.2-1B, and OPT-1.3B, achieving performance comparable to gradient-based methods (EAP, EAP-IG). The same directions that identify circuit components also enable controlled steering—geometric steering reaches 69.8% emotion-classification accuracy vs. 53.1% for instruction prompting, while preserving factual accuracy (89.6% vs. 91.2% baseline)—and can be extended to discover feature circuits for emotions, languages, and personas via prompt engineering alone, without task-specific datasets.

Continuous Adversarial Text Representation Learning for Affective Recognition
Seungah Son, Andrés Sáurez, Dongsoo Har ICAIIC 2025
A framework for enhancing emotion-aware embeddings in transformer-based models. Introduces continuous valence-arousal labels to guide contrastive learning (replacing coarse polarity labels) and a gradient-based dynamic token perturbation mechanism that targets sentiment-relevant tokens. Achieves up to 15.5% improvement on emotion classification benchmarks over baseline models, with well-separated emotional clusters validated through alignment-uniformity analysis.

About

I recently completed my MS in the Robotics Program at KAIST, where I researched mechanistic interpretability of transformer models. My work has centered on how linear structure emerges in transformer representations, how it lets us trace specific computations through the network, and what its underlying geometry reveals about how these models work.

Before KAIST, I worked as a software engineer at TransUnion (backend, monolith-to-microservices migration), Upwork (frontend performance and internationalization), and ValueLabs (Java application modernization). I hold a degree in mechanical engineering and transitioned into machine learning research driven by an interest in understanding how intelligent systems work from the inside out. Originally from Costa Rica.

I'm looking for research positions in mechanistic interpretability—in industry labs or PhD programs—where I can continue developing geometric methods for understanding and controlling large language models.
